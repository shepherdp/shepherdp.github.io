The 2/3 Problem

I put an article out on Medium that I hope to continually update. It describes a phenomenon I've seen many examples of in several different places. The essence of the 2/3 Problem is this: some problems exist that are really easy to solve when n = 2, but they become VERY hard to solve when n = 3, whatever n happens to be in the case at hand. Three notable examples are the Three Body Problem out of physics, 3-SAT out of computer science, and Fermat's Last Theorem from mathematics.

All three of these problems are parameterized by a number n - for the first, it is the number of bodies; for the second, it is the number of variables per clause; and for the third, it is the value of the exponent. In all three cases, if you set n = 2 in their respective contexts, these three problems are incredibly easy to solve. As soon as n = 3, they become incredibly difficult (or impossible) to solve.

Please take a minute to read through my article here and learn about a few of these problems. Who knows? Maybe you know of your own 2/3 Problem! I would love to hear about it.

Simple Machine Learning: Teaching a Computer to Spot Random Strings

A short while ago, I was working on a project for a company that involved some general web scraping. In essence, I would go through a few public databases and collect information about companies that had been users of various financial instruments. I would then scour the web for each of these companies to gather other public information about them - mostly things like business addresses and company websites. In order to keep my searches broad so as not to miss any juicy data, I had to gather a lot of raw data that turned out to be garbage and needed to be gone through and cleaned out.

The nature of this "garbage" was generally strings representing URLs that turned out to just be random character strings I had picked up off of some website during the search. I would have plenty of strings like "aHH6tgI.com" in my data set, and needed to be rid of them because they were cluttering up my data space. On the one hand, I did NOT want to filter these things manually. On the other hand, I also didn't want to narrow my tool's search capabilities because it was gathering the data I needed it to.

In response to this, I created a miniature machine learning model, trained it on valid strings, and then allowed it to do my data cleansing for me. I ran several tests against data I had labeled to measure its performance, and it was able to clean up far over 95% of the "garbage" in my data, and lost less than 1% of the good data. Frankly, even the cases in which good data was filtered out were understandable, given the individual strings that didn't make it through the filter.

To see a small example of data science and ML in action, feel free to take a look through the article here. It may inspire you to pick up a little machine learning yourself! It's much more approachable than people give it credit for.
